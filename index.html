  • Abstract

    DNA origami is known as an innovative technology that can build various 2D and 3D nanostructures using DNA strands. In conventional procedure, DNA origami is folded in bulk solution such as in a test tube. Here, we present our project “SynthePHERE”, which aims at the folding of DNA origami nanostructures in a small compartment such as in a liposome.

    As a first step, giant unilamellar vesicles (GUVs) containing scaffold and staple　strands are prepared. The DNA origami nanostructures are folded in GUVs with the temperature control of the solution. Since scaffold and staple strands are inside of a small reaction system of GUVs, faster and efficient DNA origami folding is expected.

    Additionally, introduction of staple strands into GUVs through the pores on the membrane surfaces is carried out. A membrane protein streptolysin O(SLO) is used to form pores with ≈ 27 nm diameter on GUV membrane surfaces. Also, SLO can get sealed when calcium ions are added. By closing SLO pores, scaffold and staple strands are expected to be entrapped in GUVs, allowing to fold DNA origami nanostructures inside GUVs.

    By introducing different staple strands into a small compartment, various DNA nanostructures can be folded as a response. This input-output system would be applied to liposome-type molecular robots that can respond to outer environments, which could be used as drug delivery system.

  • Introduction

    Modern robots receive a large amount of data from the external environment (via sensors) and operate based on the feedback. In order for robots to be multifunctional and high-performance, the ability of high-speed mass data processing is required. However, molecular robots today are not advanced enough to handle large amounts of data, nor do they have the way to accelerate the process without external influence. In our project, we used liposomes as containers that separate the inner solution from the outer solution by lipid bilayers. DNAorigami was used as a technology that uses staples as inputs and scaffolds as machine models that respond to inputs to resemble molecular robots. DNA origami is a nanoscale folding technology that enables the construction of two- or three- dimensional nanostructures with arbitrary shapes. Two types of DNA strands, scaffolds and staples are used for construction of DNA origami nanostructures. Scaffolds are long single stranded DNA with thousands of bases, while staples are short DNA strands that bind to corresponding areas of scaffolds. Our goal is to build liposome-type molecular robots that receive a large amount of data and provide rapid feedback. It is expected that the DNA origami folding process is much more efficient in small compartments such as liposomes than in bulk solution.

  • Design

    To prove that DNA origami nanostructure can be folded in liposomes, the DNA origami nanostructure must be larger than the hole diameter on the liposome. In addition, we selected a three dimensional shape for DNA origami design to prove that more complex structure can be folded in the liposome. It means artificial cells can gain functionality by absorbing scaffold and staple from outside and folding even complex DNA origami structures(Fig.1.a). In future experience, the DNA origami structure must be observed in liposomes to prove by using fluorescence molecules that it was absorbed and folded in them. Because of this, the staple chains at any position in the surface of the structure must be replaceable with fluorescent alleys while maintaining stability(Fig.1.b to d).
